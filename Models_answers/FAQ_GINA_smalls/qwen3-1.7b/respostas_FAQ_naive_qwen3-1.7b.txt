### What is Asthma?  
Asthma is a chronic respiratory disease characterized by **airway hyperresponsiveness**, **chronic inflammation**, and **variable expiratory airflow limitation**. It is a heterogeneous condition, meaning it can have diverse underlying causes and manifestations. Key features include:  

1. **Symptoms**:  
   - Variable respiratory symptoms such as **wheezing**, **shortness of breath**, **chest tightness**, and **cough**.  
   - Symptoms may worsen during **exercise**, **allergen exposure**, or **cold air**, and improve with **inhaled corticosteroids (ICS)**.  

2. **Diagnostic Criteria**:  
   - **Recurrent acute wheezing episodes** with or without asthma-like symptoms.  
   - **Confirmed variable expiratory airflow limitation** (e.g., spirometry or PEF measurements).  
   - **Differential diagnosis** to rule out other conditions (e.g., COPD, occupational asthma).  

3. **Types of Asthma**:  
   - **Allergic Asthma**: Linked to **allergies** (e.g., eczema, food allergies), with **eosinophilic airway inflammation**.  
   - **Non-Allergic Asthma**: Associated with **non-allergic triggers** (e.g., irritants, exercise), with **neutrophilic airway inflammation**.  
   - **Cough Variant Asthma**: Characterized by **persistent cough** without wheezing.  
   - **Occupational Asthma**: Triggered by workplace exposures (e.g., chemicals, allergens).  

4. **Diagnosis**:  
   - **Lung function tests** (e.g., spirometry, PEF) are critical, especially in patients without access to spirometry.  
   - **Bronchodilator responsiveness** (e.g., reversibility) confirms airway obstruction.  
   - **Clinical history** and **physical examination** help differentiate asthma from other conditions (e.g., COPD, heart failure).  

5. **Management**:  
   - **ICS-containing treatment** is often required for symptom control, with adjustments based on **lung function variability** and **response to treatment**.  
   - **Biologic therapies** (e.g., monoclonal antibodies) may be used in severe or refractory cases.  

### Key Takeaways  
Asthma is a **chronic, heterogeneous condition** with **variable symptoms** and **airway inflammation**. It requires **accurate diagnosis** (via spirometry or PEF) and **targeted treatment** to manage symptoms and prevent exacerbations. The distinction between asthma and other respiratory conditions (e.g., COPD, heart failure) is critical for proper care.

### Inflammation in the Airways of Asthma  

Inflammation in the airways is a hallmark of asthma, characterized by chronic airway inflammation that persists even when symptoms are absent or lung function is normal. This inflammation is a key feature of asthma and is often classified as **Type 2 inflammation**, which involves the adaptive immune system and is associated with the presence of cytokines like interleukin (IL)-4, IL-5, and IL-13.  

#### Key Aspects of Airway Inflammation in Asthma:  
1. **Types of Inflammation**:  
   - **Type 2 Inflammation**:  
     - Dominant in allergic asthma, characterized by **eosinophilic airway inflammation**.  
     - Marked by elevated blood eosinophils, fractional exhaled nitric oxide (FeNO), and sputum eosinophils.  
     - Often responds well to inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) but may be **relatively refractory** to high-dose ICS in severe asthma.  
   - **Non-Allergic Asthma**:  
     - May involve **neutrophilic or paucigranulocytic inflammation**.  
     - Less responsive to ICS and may require alternative treatments.  

2. **Biomarkers of Inflammation**:  
   - **Eosinophils**: Elevated levels (e.g., ≥150/µL) indicate Type 2 inflammation, though a single low count does not rule out asthma.  
   - **FeNO (Fractional Exhaled Nitric Oxide)**: Elevated levels (e.g., ≥20 ppb) are strongly associated with Type 2 inflammation.  
   - **Sputum Eosinophils**: Elevated levels suggest allergic asthma.  

3. **Role in Disease Pathogenesis**:  
   - Airway inflammation contributes to **airflow limitation**, **airway remodeling**, and **chronic symptoms**.  
   - Persistent inflammation may lead to **airway wall remodeling**, which is a key factor in **difficult-to-treat asthma**.  

4. **Clinical Implications**:  
   - Biomarkers like FeNO and eosinophils are critical for diagnosing Type 2 asthma, especially in patients with **persistent airflow limitation** or **obesity**.  
   - In severe asthma, inflammation may be **refractory to high-dose ICS** and may require **biologic therapies** (e.g., dupilumab, omalizumab) targeting specific inflammatory pathways.  

5. **Management Strategies**:  
   - **ICS** is the first-line treatment for inflammation, but **inhaled corticosteroid (ICS)-formoterol** may be required for patients with **persistent airflow limitation** or **refractory inflammation**.  
   - **Biomarker-guided treatment** (e.g., FeNO ≥20 ppb) helps identify patients who may benefit from biologic therapy.

### What Happens During an Asthma Attack?

An asthma attack is an episode of worsening respiratory symptoms characterized by **wheezing, shortness of breath, coughing, and chest tightness**. The attack is typically triggered by **allergens, cold air, exercise, or respiratory infections**. Here's a structured overview of the process:

---

#### **1. Clinical Features and Symptoms**
- **Wheezing**: A high-pitched, whistling sound during breathing, often audible with a stethoscope. It is caused by **airway narrowing** (bronchoconstriction).
- **Shortness of Breath (Dyspnea)**: Difficulty breathing, often accompanied by **tachycardia** (rapid heart rate) or **tachypnea** (rapid breathing).
- **Coughing**: Dry or productive (with mucus), often worse at night or during sleep.
- **Chest Tightness**: A feeling of pressure or constriction in the chest.
- **Reduced Lung Function**: Measured via **peak expiratory flow (PEF)** or **forced expiratory volume in 1 second (FEV1)**, which may drop significantly.

---

#### **2. Triggers**
- **Allergens**: Pollen, dust mites, pet dander, or mold.
- **Cold Air**: Sudden exposure to cold air can trigger bronchoconstriction.
- **Exercise**: Physical activity can worsen symptoms in individuals with **asthma and exercise-induced bronchoconstriction**.
- **Infections**: Viral respiratory infections (e.g., rhinovirus, influenza) can trigger exacerbations.
- **Stress or Emotional Stress**: Can trigger **bronchoconstriction**.

---

#### **3. Diagnostic Criteria**
- **Recurrence of Asthma-Like Symptoms**: At least two episodes (lasting >24 hours) with **wheezing** during episodes or **interval symptoms** (between episodes).
- **Confirmable Airway Obstruction**: Wheezing confirmed during an episode or **PEF reversibility** (response to bronchodilators).
- **Clinical Response**: Improvement in symptoms within 20–60 minutes after **SABA (short-acting beta-2 agonist)** or **ICS (inhaled corticosteroid)**.

---

#### **4. Management and Treatment**

### Controllers in Asthma Management  
Controllers are medications used to manage asthma symptoms and prevent exacerbations. They are essential for maintaining long-term control and reducing the risk of future asthma attacks. The choice of controller depends on factors such as age, adherence, and risk factors, and is often guided by guidelines like GINA (Global Initiative for Asthma).  

#### Key Types of Controllers  
1. **Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS)**  
   - **Preferred controllers**: ICS (e.g., budesonide-formoterol, beclometasone-formoterol) are the primary controllers for most patients. They reduce airway inflammation and are effective in controlling symptoms.  
   - **Usage**: ICS is delivered via pressurized metered-dose inhalers (pMDIs) with spacers, and the preferred device is a pMDI with a mouthpiece for children aged 3–5 years.  
   - **Step-up considerations**: If a child’s symptoms are uncontrolled, ICS doses may be increased to medium (Step 4) or combined with long-acting beta-2 agonists (LABA) in some cases.  

2. **Long-Acting Beta-2 Agonists (LABA)**  
   - **Role**: LABA is used in combination with ICS (e.g., ICS-formoterol) to improve symptom control. It is often prescribed for patients with persistent airflow limitation.  
   - **Usage**: LABA is used as a reliever in combination with ICS, and its efficacy is supported by clinical trials.  

3. **Short-Acting Beta-2 Agonists (SABA)**  
   - **Role**: SABA (e.g., salbutamol) is used for acute symptom relief but is not a controller. It is prescribed as needed (as-needed SABA) to manage breakthrough symptoms.  
   - **Limitation**: SABA is not a long-term controller and is used only for acute episodes.  

4. **Add-On Therapies**  
   - **Biologics**: For severe asthma, biologics (e.g., mepolizumab) may be used in combination with ICS to reduce exacerbations.  
   - **Add-on controllers**: In some cases, additional controllers (e.g., ICS-LABA or ICS-SABA) may be prescribed to optimize symptom control.  

#### Key Considerations  
- **Adherence**: Proper adherence to controller therapy is critical. Poor adherence can lead to uncontrolled symptoms and exacerbations.  
- **Inhaler Technique**: Correct use of inhalers (e.g., spacer devices) is essential to ensure effective delivery of medication.  
- **Risk Factors**: Controllers are tailored to individual risk factors, such as comorbidities, environmental exposures, and adherence issues.  
- **Step-Up/Step-Down**: Controllers are adjusted based on symptom control and response. For example, if a child’s symptoms remain uncontrolled, ICS doses may be increased or combined with LABA.

RESPOSTA:
### Differences Between Asthma and COPD

Asthma and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) are both chronic respiratory conditions, but they differ in their causes, symptoms, diagnostic criteria, and management approaches. Here’s a structured comparison:

---

**1. **Symptoms and Triggers**  
- **Asthma**: Characterized by **variable expiratory airflow** (e.g., wheezing, shortness of breath, cough, and chest tightness), often triggered by allergens, irritants, exercise, or viral infections. Symptoms may worsen at night or during activity.  
- **COPD**: Marked by **persistent airflow limitation** due to chronic airway inflammation and emphysema. Symptoms include chronic cough, sputum production, and dyspnea, often exacerbated by smoking or environmental pollutants. COPD progresses over time, with symptoms worsening progressively.  

---

**2. **Diagnostic Criteria**  
- **Asthma**: Diagnosed based on **variable expiratory airflow** (e.g., FEV1/FVC ratios <70% or ≥12% improvement in PEF after bronchodilator use) and the presence of **atopic or non-atopic asthma**. Asthma and COPD can overlap, but the diagnostic criteria differ in how they define airflow limitation.  
- **COPD**: Confirmed by **persistent airflow limitation** (post-bronchodilation FEV1/FVC <70%) and a history of **smoking or environmental exposures**. COPD is often associated with **emphysema** and **airway wall thickening**, while asthma may involve **bronchitis** or **alveolar destruction**.  

---

**3. **Pathophysiology**  
- **Asthma**: A **heterogeneous condition** with airway hyperresponsiveness and chronic inflammation. It is often **allergic** (e.g., eosinophilic airway inflammation) or **non-allergic** (e.g., neutrophilic inflammation).  
- **COPD**: A **progressive lung disease** caused by **airway inflammation and emphysema**, often due to **smoking** or **air pollution**. It involves **airway remodeling** and **alveolar destruction**, leading to irreversible airflow limitation.
