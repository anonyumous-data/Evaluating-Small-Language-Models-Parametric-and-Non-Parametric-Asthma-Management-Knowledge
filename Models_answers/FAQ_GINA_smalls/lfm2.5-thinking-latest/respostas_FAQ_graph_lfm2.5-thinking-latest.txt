3. **Mucus Production**: Increased mucus production further obstructs airflow, worsening symptoms.

4. **Immune Cell Activity**: Immune cells (e.g., T-cells, eosinophils) may contribute to chronic inflammation, leading to persistent airway narrowing and increased sensitivity.

In asthma, this inflammation often becomes **chronic**, leading to long-term damage and reduced lung function. While inflammation is protective against pathogens, excessive or prolonged activation can worsen symptoms and complicate treatment. Managing inflammation (e.g., with anti-inflammatory drugs) is key to controlling asthma. 

In summary, inflammation in asthma drives the physical changes that define the condition but also highlights the need for balanced immune regulation.
