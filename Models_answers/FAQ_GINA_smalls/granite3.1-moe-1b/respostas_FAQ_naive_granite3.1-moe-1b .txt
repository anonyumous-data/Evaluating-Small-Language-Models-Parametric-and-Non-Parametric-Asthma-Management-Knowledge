   - Shortness of breath or difficulty breathing
   - Frequent or recurrent asthma attacks

   - Asthma symptoms frequency
   - Asthma control rating (e.g., asthma-related quality of life (ARQL))
   - Asthma medication adherence
   - Asthma-related hospitalizations and emergency department visits

8. **Asthma Uncontrolled**: Asthma uncontrolled is considered to be asthma for which at least one of the following conditions is true:
   - Symptoms persist for more than 12 months, even with treatment

   - Asthma symptoms and exacerbations cause significant distress or impairment in daily activities

PROMPT 77:
the next question is about asthma, What is the difference between asthma and COPD (chronic obstructive lung disease)?

RESPOSTA:
Asthma and Chronic Obstructive Lung Disease (COPD) are both respiratory conditions, but they have distinct differences:

1. **Pathophysiology**: In asthma, the airways are inflamed, and the airway muscles tighten, causing the airways to narrow and producing wheezing, coughing, and chest tightness. In COPD, the airways become damaged and narrowed due to a combination of factors like cigarette smoke, air pollution, and genetics. This damage leads to persistent inflammation, smooth muscle hyperplasia, and mucus hypersecretion, causing chronic airflow obstruction.

2. **Symptoms**: The primary symptoms of asthma include wheezing, coughing, chest tightness, shortness of breath, and sneezing. In COPD, the symptoms often include shortness of breath, chronic cough, and wheezing, with symptoms becoming more severe and interfering with daily activities over time.

3. **Severity and Progression**: Asthma is generally a less severe condition compared to COPD. Asthma symptoms can be managed with medications and lifestyle modifications, and it often responds well to bronchodilators and corticosteroids. On the other hand, COPD progresses more rapidly, with symptoms becoming more severe over time and affecting quality of life significantly.

4. **Prognosis**: The prognosis for asthma varies among individuals. While some people with asthma may have mild symptoms and require occasional interventions, others may develop severe asthma, which can lead to life-threatening symptoms and complications. In COPD, the prognosis is generally more favorable, with many people experiencing a significant improvement in their condition with appropriate treatment.

5. **Complications**: Both asthma and COPD can lead to complications, such as airway remodeling, bronchial obstruction, and respiratory infections. However, COPD is often associated with more severe complications, such as exacerbations of asthma leading to acute exacerbations, and increased mortality rates compared to asthma.

In summary, while both asthma and COPD are respiratory conditions, they differ in their pathophysiology, symptoms, severity, prognosis, and complications. Asthma is generally milder and less severe, while COPD is more chronic, progressive, and associated with a higher risk of respiratory infections and increased mortality.
